# Employee-Ownership Handbook

> **Audience:** Every member of the company — reviewers, trainers,
> stewards, founders, and the people considering joining us.
>
> **Outcome:** A shared understanding of what employee ownership *means*
> at Plan-Examiner: what changes when you become an owner, what doesn't,
> and what we expect of one another.

> **Important:** This handbook describes our chosen *model and values*.
> The legal instruments — articles of incorporation, bylaws, equity
> plans, tax structure — vary by jurisdiction and by the legal entity
> we use (cooperative, ESOP, employee-ownership trust, or worker-owned
> LLC). Nothing in this document is legal, tax, or investment advice.
> Pair this handbook with counsel licensed in the relevant jurisdiction.

---

## 1. Why employee ownership

Employee ownership is not a perk; it is the operating system. We chose
it for four reasons:

1. **Better craft.** Owners do better work than employees. Plan review
   is craft work; the leverage of an owner-mindset is enormous.
2. **A more durable privacy promise.** Plans don't leave the browser
   because the architecture forbids it — *and* because the people who
   could change that architecture are the same people who would own
   the consequences.
3. **Long-horizon thinking.** Distributed ownership biases toward
   decisions that pay back in years, not quarters.
4. **A fairer share of value.** Reviewers create the value. They
   should share in it.

---

## 2. The four owner archetypes

These describe **roles in the company**, not legal share classes. (For
share classes, see [Governance Bylaws Template](governance-bylaws-template.md).)

| Archetype | Role | Typical responsibility |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Reviewer-Owner** | Day-to-day reviewer, fully owner-class | Direct work + 1 owner activity per quarter |
| **Trainer-Owner** | Trainer with owner status | Direct work + a trainer cohort per year |
| **Steward-Owner** | Owner serving in a domain steward role | Domain stewardship + direct work |
| **Founding-Owner** | One of the original founders, no special governance rights | Same as above; *no* perpetual veto |

Founding-owners hold no governance powers beyond those of any other
owner. This is intentional and is enshrined in the
[bylaws template](governance-bylaws-template.md).

## 3. How you become an owner

A typical path (adjust to your specific legal vehicle):

1. **Eligibility window** — typically 90 days after start, contingent on
   passing the [onboarding milestones](../04-onboarding/new-hire-onboarding.md).
2. **Eligibility letter** — issued by the hiring manager.
3. **Owner orientation** — a 2-hour session covering bylaws, owners'
   rights and obligations, and basic finance literacy for our model.
4. **Owner vote** — at the next quarterly owners' meeting, existing
   owners vote on the new owner per the bylaws (typical: simple
   majority; *no* unanimous-consent requirement, which would create
   pocket vetoes).
5. **Buy-in or grant** — depending on the legal vehicle, the new owner
   either buys in (often via payroll deduction over time) or receives
   a grant (vested over a published schedule). The
   [equity guide](profit-sharing-and-equity.md) covers this in detail.
6. **Welcome.** You are an owner. We expect both more *and* less of
   you — more responsibility, less hierarchy.

## 4. What changes when you become an owner

### 4.1 What you gain

- **A vote** at the owners' meeting on bylaws-level matters.
- **A share** of profit per the [profit-sharing formula](profit-sharing-and-equity.md).
- **Standing** to demand a vote on a decision you believe was made at
  the wrong rung (see [Leadership Handbook §7.3](../05-executive/executive-leadership-handbook.md#73-the-any-owner-can-demand-a-vote-rule)).
- **Read access** to the financial and operational dashboard.
- **Eligibility** for steward roles.

### 4.2 What you take on

- **Showing up.** Owners attend the quarterly owners' meeting absent
  good cause. Two consecutive misses without notice triggers a check-in.
- **Reading the financials.** Not deeply, just regularly enough to
  participate. The finance steward is responsible for making them
  legible.
- **One owner activity per quarter.** Examples: leading a workshop
  segment, mentoring a new hire, owning a runbook update, reviewing a
  PR backlog. Stewards count their stewardship.
- **Honest dissent.** Silence in meetings followed by sniping after is
  the failure mode owners must reject. Disagree in the room, in
  writing, on the record.

### 4.3 What does *not* change

- You are still paid as a reviewer for reviewer work. Profit-sharing
  is *on top of* salary, not in lieu.
- You are not the boss of anyone you weren't the boss of yesterday.
- You don't gain authority over operational decisions that aren't
  yours to make. Owner ≠ everyone-decides-everything; owner = you have
  voice and standing where it matters.

## 5. The owners' rhythm

| Cadence | Activity | Required attendance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Monthly | Finance update (read-only memo) | Read; respond if needed |
| Quarterly | Owners' meeting | Yes (absent good cause) |
| Annually | Owners' AGM (compensation, profit-sharing, bylaws review) | Yes (absent excused absence) |
| Ad hoc | Any-owner-demanded vote (rare) | Yes if relevant |

### 5.1 The quarterly owners' meeting

A typical agenda:

| Time | Item |
| --- | --- |
| 0:00 | Welcome + roll + minutes from prior meeting |
| 0:10 | Finance update by the finance steward |
| 0:25 | Operations update by the operations steward |
| 0:40 | One *deep dive* — rotates each quarter (people, technology, governance, mission) |
| 1:00 | Decisions tabled at the prior meeting; votes taken |
| 1:20 | New business |
| 1:40 | Open mic — any owner, any topic |
| 1:55 | Close + minutes assigned |

Minutes are written, signed by two owners (one not on the steward
council), and archived.

### 5.2 The annual AGM

Adds: compensation review, bylaws review, election of any rotating
steward roles, vote on the community-contribution recipient, and
acceptance of the [annual mission letter](../06-operations/sustainability-and-stewardship.md#35-the-annual-mission-letter).

## 6. Conflict, dissent, and supermajority votes

### 6.1 Default decision rule
Most owner votes are **simple majority** of owners present, quorum being
50%+1 of all owners.

### 6.2 Supermajority categories
The following decisions require a **two-thirds supermajority** of all
owners (not just present), per the bylaws:

- Any change that would route plan data through Plan-Examiner-controlled
  servers
- Any closing of source code we ship today
- Sale of the company or any equivalent change of control
- Material reductions to the profit-sharing share of net profit
- Bylaws amendments
- Removal of an owner

These are the categories that, if eroded, would change *what we are*.
Supermajority is the friction that protects them.

### 6.3 Dissent on the record

Any owner can attach a written dissent to any decision. Dissents are
archived with the minutes. We do not count dissent against the dissenter
in any review, evaluation, or compensation conversation.

### 6.4 Removal of an owner

Removal is rare and bylaws-defined. The process protects the owner
against capricious removal: written notice, opportunity to respond, a
supermajority vote, and a fair buy-out per the
[equity guide](profit-sharing-and-equity.md). It is, deliberately,
hard.

## 7. Becoming, staying, and leaving

### 7.1 Becoming
Covered in §3.

### 7.2 Staying
- Active employment is generally required to remain a current owner.
  Leaves of absence per policy do not threaten ownership.
- Ownership is *not* automatically perpetual after departure; see §7.3.

### 7.3 Leaving
- Voluntary departure: a published buy-out schedule applies. No
  surprises.
- Involuntary departure (poor fit, misconduct, layoff): the same buy-out
  schedule applies, with terms varying by cause as defined in the
  bylaws. We do **not** punish ownership departure with worse buy-out
  terms unless cause is established under the disciplinary process.
- We do not write non-competes that prevent former owners from
  practicing plan review elsewhere. We may write narrow
  confidentiality and IP terms.

## 8. The owner's compact

We sign this when we become owners. Read it now even if you're not
yet an owner.

> *I commit to:*
> 1. *Doing the work in the open.*
> 2. *Treating dissent as data, not as disloyalty.*
> 3. *Pulling weight beyond my own seat — at least one owner activity
>    per quarter.*
> 4. *Reading the finances honestly enough to vote with care.*
> 5. *Holding the privacy promise as architecture, not as marketing.*
> 6. *Mentoring the next reviewer behind me.*
> 7. *Leaving the company better than I found it.*

## 9. FAQ

**"Do I have to take on more work to become an owner?"**
You take on more *responsibility* — one owner activity per quarter —
not necessarily more hours. Stewards count their stewardship; trainers
count their cohorts; reviewers can pick contribution work that fits
their interest.

**"What if I don't want to be an owner?"**
You don't have to be. Non-owner reviewers are valued and paid
competitively. Ownership is for those who want voice in the
governance of the company.

**"Does ownership pay better than non-ownership?"**
Salary is the same for the same role. Profit-sharing is on top, paid
to owners only. In good years it is meaningful; in bad years it can be
zero. See the [equity guide](profit-sharing-and-equity.md).

**"Can owners get fired?"**
Yes. Ownership doesn't grant immunity. It does grant a fair buy-out and
a defined removal process — see §6.4 and §7.3.

**"What if I disagree with an owners' vote?"**
You attach a written dissent (§6.3). It's archived. We do not penalize
dissenters. The vote stands, but the record is honest.

**"Can outside investors own us?"**
Generally no — see [governance bylaws template](governance-bylaws-template.md)
for the exact restriction. Outside *non-equity* funding (loans,
grants) is allowed under tighter conditions.
