# Executive Leadership Handbook

> **Audience:** People exercising executive responsibility — founders,
> stewards, lead trainers, board members. In an employee-owned
> organization, "executive" is a hat, not a class.
>
> **Outcome:** Shared expectations and language for the people who
> steward Plan-Examiner the company.

---

## 1. The leader's job in three lines

1. Make sure the work continues to deserve the trust placed in it.
2. Make sure the people doing the work are growing and being treated
   well.
3. Make sure tomorrow exists — finances, safety, mission alignment —
   so 1 and 2 can keep happening.

If you cannot trace what you're doing today back to one of those three,
delegate it or stop doing it.

---

## 2. The leadership domains

We organize executive responsibility around six **domains**. Each domain
has a **steward** — a person, not a department. Stewards are confirmed
by the [governance process](../07-employee-ownership/governance-bylaws-template.md).

| Domain | What it owns | Common artifacts |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Mission & strategy** | What we do, what we don't do, where we go next | Annual letter, roadmap |
| **People & culture** | Hiring, onboarding, learning, conflict, equity | Hiring playbook, onboarding plan, retro reports |
| **Operations** | Day-to-day delivery, quality, capacity, vendors | [Operations Manual](../06-operations/company-operations-manual.md), runbooks |
| **Finance** | Cash, pricing, profit-sharing, taxes | Quarterly statements, [profit-sharing guide](../07-employee-ownership/profit-sharing-and-equity.md) |
| **Technology** | Codebase, infra, security, privacy | Repo, [Security policy](../../../SECURITY.md), service-worker config |
| **Governance** | Bylaws, ownership, board, votes, disputes | [Governance bylaws template](../07-employee-ownership/governance-bylaws-template.md) |

A steward is **first among equals** in their domain — not a manager.
Decisions in the domain are usually steward-led; significant decisions
require the [decision-rights ladder](#4-decision-rights-ladder).

## 3. The leader's calendar (rough sizing)

If you can't account for your time across these buckets, you're
probably leaking time. Adjust to your scale.

| Bucket | % | What goes here |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direct work (reviews, code, docs) | 30–50% | The leader who never does the work loses credibility |
| 1:1s | 15–25% | Onboarding new hires, supporting stewards |
| Domain stewardship | 15–25% | Your domain's artifacts and decisions |
| Cross-domain coordination | 10–15% | Standing meetings, governance |
| Outside (clients, investors, partners) | 5–15% | External work |
| Reflection / strategy | 5–10% | Reading, writing, thinking |

If "outside" creeps above 25%, you're running a sales business, not a
plan-review business. Re-balance or hand the domain to a colleague.

## 4. Decision-rights ladder

Different decisions have different blast radii. Match the decision to
the right rung.

| Rung | Examples | Who decides | Required artifact |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 — Personal | Pick your editor, your working hours | Yourself | None |
| 2 — Project | Whether to flag a specific finding | Reviewer of record | Verbose log |
| 3 — Operational | A vendor change under threshold; a new training time slot | Domain steward | Note in the relevant runbook |
| 4 — Cross-domain | Pricing change; new partner relationship | Two stewards + 1 senior reviewer | Written proposal + retro after |
| 5 — Governance | Bylaws, profit-sharing changes, new owner-class | All owners per [bylaws](../07-employee-ownership/governance-bylaws-template.md) | Notice + meeting + recorded vote |
| 6 — Mission | Pivot the business; sell the company | All owners + supermajority | Same + 30-day deliberation period |

> **Rule of thumb:** when in doubt, escalate one rung *and* invite the
> peer who would be most surprised to find out later. Most leadership
> failures are people deciding at rung N when rung N+1 was warranted.

## 5. Communication norms

### 5.1 Default to writing
Important decisions go in writing. Verbal-only decisions evaporate.
"Notes from the call" emails are fine; the point is durability.

### 5.2 Default to public-internal
Within the company, default visibility is "everyone." Confidential is
the exception, not the rule. This is non-negotiable in an
employee-owned model: owners are entitled to context.

### 5.3 Default to short
A two-paragraph memo beats an unread ten-pager. If your memo is long
because the topic is complex, lead with a TL;DR.

### 5.4 Default to slow on tone, fast on facts
Disagreements escalate when tone is treated as urgent. Get the facts on
the table fast; let the tone settle on its own clock.

### 5.5 Default to retro
Every significant decision gets a 1-paragraph retro 30 days later:
*what did we decide, what happened, what would we do differently?*
File it in the relevant domain's retro folder.

## 6. Quarterly leadership rhythm

| Cadence | Activity | Owner |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Weekly | Steward sync (30 min) | Mission steward |
| Bi-weekly | Quality sampling review | Operations steward |
| Monthly | Finance update to all owners | Finance steward |
| Monthly | Hiring & onboarding pipeline review | People steward |
| Quarterly | Owners' meeting (see [bylaws](../07-employee-ownership/governance-bylaws-template.md)) | Governance steward |
| Quarterly | KPI re-baseline (see [Executive Briefing §B5](executive-briefing.md#b5-kpi-definitions-precise)) | Operations steward |
| Annually | Mission letter + roadmap | Mission steward |
| Annually | Compensation & profit-sharing recalibration | Finance + Governance |
| Annually | Trainer cohort planning | People steward |
| Annually | Independent review of privacy posture | Tech steward |

Adapt cadence to scale. The point is *every domain has a heartbeat*,
not that the table is gospel.

## 7. Conflict and escalation

Conflict is normal. Avoidable conflict is bad management.

### 7.1 The escalation path

```
Direct conversation
       │
       ▼ (does not resolve in two attempts)
Domain steward
       │
       ▼ (still unresolved)
Cross-domain steward council
       │
       ▼ (rare)
Owners' meeting / governance vote
```

Anyone can skip a level if a step is unsafe (harassment, retaliation,
financial impropriety). The relevant policy is the
[Code of Conduct](../../../CODE_OF_CONDUCT.md) and (for security
matters) [SECURITY.md](../../../SECURITY.md).

### 7.2 Rules every steward enforces
- **No public adversarial litigation of personnel disputes.** They are
  handled in the relevant rung above, in writing, with the affected
  parties present.
- **Document the decision and the reasoning.** "We decided X for
  reasons Y and Z, dissent noted from W."
- **Retro every escalation.** A pattern of escalations from one team is
  a signal, not a coincidence.

### 7.3 The "any owner can demand a vote" rule
In an employee-owned org, any owner can demand a vote on a decision
they believe was made at the wrong rung. This is **not** unlimited; the
owners' meeting may rule the demand frivolous, but the demand itself
must be heard. This is documented in the [bylaws template](../07-employee-ownership/governance-bylaws-template.md).

## 8. Leading through change

The two changes that test leadership most:

1. **A bad month.** Delivery slipped, a letter went out wrong, a key
   reviewer left. Lead with: facts → impact → ownership of next steps
   → support for the affected. Do **not** lead with reassurance.
2. **A surprisingly good month.** New revenue, big partner deal,
   capacity full. Lead with: facts → sustainability question → don't
   over-extend. The single fastest way to break a co-op is to scale
   beyond what trainers can sustain.

## 9. The leader's annual self-review

A short instrument every steward uses on themselves once a year. Score
1–5, write a sentence each.

1. Did I do enough direct work to remain credible to my colleagues?
2. Did I grow at least one person below me to take on more
   responsibility?
3. Did I handle at least one hard conversation I would have preferred
   to avoid?
4. Did I make at least one decision that I later, in retro, judged
   wrong, *and* document it?
5. Did I model the privacy and ownership values I'm asking others to
   model?
6. Did I take enough rest to do this for another year?

A score of "5 across the board" is suspicious. Real leaders rate
themselves honestly, in front of peers, every year.
